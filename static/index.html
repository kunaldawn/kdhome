KD's Homebrew Digital Archive — a home-grown mirror of the public internet. 12 TB of preserved knowledge served from a homelab cluster: two Raspberry Pi 4 nodes (8 GB) plus an N150 mini-PC (12 GB), on a 5-port switch fed from a single 65 W USB-C adapter. 4x 2 TB HDDs + 2x 2 TB SSDs hot, with a 12 TB external HDD for cold backup. About 30 watts total, engineered to run off-grid on a modest solar rig. Seven sub-archives in total: 36 offline wikis including Wikipedia, Stack Overflow, ArchWiki, iFixit, and Project Gutenberg; 23,000+ PDFs across 800 GB; dedicated archives of operating system images and drivers, vintage CD/DVD software and abandonware, chiptune and keygen music, curated YouTube mirrors, and audiobooks. A shelf in a house, not a rack in a data centre. Developed by Kunal Dawn with AI assistance.

Wiki Archive

                // Purpose
                [+]
              

              

                
                  A shelf in a house, not a rack in a data centre. This is a personal collection — retro
                  computing, chiptunes, dead magazines, abandonware, dusty journals, and manuals for
                  chips that stopped being made decades ago. Not a full-blown internet archival
                  project; just the stuff one person didn't want to lose, kept around because it's
                  worth keeping.



                  I hoard far more than what you see here. A handful of archives get published publicly
                  as a fallback: if the original source ever goes dark, a copy is still reachable
                  here. No promises, no SLA, no uptime guarantees — just a shelf that happens to be
                  online, for as long as it stays online. I homebrew everything — beer, electronics,
                  software, ideas, and apparently, archives — and I plan to keep this one up for as
                  long as I'm around to run it.



                  Gold to the few who go looking. Websites shut down,
                  servers get decommissioned, databases deprecate, and entire libraries of human work
                  quietly disappear. One shelf can't fix that — but a lot of shelves can. If something
                  here matters to you, grab it, mirror it, seed it, start your own archive.
                  Preservation scales through a lot of people doing a little of it; that's the whole
                  point of homebrew.



                  No ads. No tracking. No telemetry. No SLA. No monetization. No users. Free for all, free
                  forever. Information wants to be free — but it also wants to be
                  preserved.
                
              

            


            

              

                // Infrastructure
                [+]
              

              

                
                  The archive runs on a small homelab cluster: two Raspberry Pi 4 (8 GB)
                  nodes for the services and scraping, plus an N150 mini-PC (12 GB) for
                  heavier indexing. They share a 5-port switch and the whole rig is fed
                  from a single 65 W USB-C power adapter. Storage is
                  4×2 TB HDDs + 2×2 TB SSDs for the hot working set, with a
                  12 TB external HDD kept offline for cold backups. The stack pulls about
                  30 W total — deliberately engineered to run off-grid on a modest solar
                  rig. No cloud, no CDN, no SLA; when the battery dies the site goes with it. Preservation
                  doesn't require a data centre — just determination, a shelf, and enough panels to cover
                  the draw.
