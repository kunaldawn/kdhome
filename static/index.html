Featured Archives

            

          


          

            

              
                
Wiki Archive

                
119 offline wikis in ZIM — Wikipedia family, 20 Stack Exchange sites (Stack Overflow, MathOverflow), 30+ DevDocs developer references, 13 LibreTexts academic libraries, Khan Academy, iFixit, Project Gutenberg, ArchWiki, MDWiki medical, WikiCiv survival, and more.

              

              
                
PDF Archive

                
23,000+ PDFs across 800 GB — vintage computer hardware manuals, decades of Byte Magazine, science journals dating to the 1890s, repair guides, MIT Press publications, and retro story books.

              

              
                
OS Archive

                
Operating system images and driver archives — bootable install media, ISOs, legacy drivers, and BIOS updates spanning decades of computing, ready when the originals disappear.

              

              
                
CD/DVD Archive

                
Vintage CD and DVD disc images — abandonware and commercial software releases that time forgot, preserved before the discs rot and the install keys are lost.

              

              
                
Chiptune Archive

                
Chiptune and keygen music — MOD, XM, S3M, and IT tracker modules, demo scene audio, and the signature soundtrack of the cracking scene. The sound of a computer showing off.

              

              
                
Tube Archive

                
Curated YouTube mirrors of computer science, hardware teardowns, engineering, math, physics, and technology deep-dives — saved before takedowns or silent deindexing make them unreachable.

              

              
                
Audiobook Archive

                
Audiobook collection spanning fiction, non-fiction, lectures, and classic literature — knowledge and storytelling for the ears, for long drives and longer nights.

              

            


          


          
          


            

              

                // Purpose
                [+]
              

              

                
                  A shelf in a house, not a rack in a data centre. This is a personal collection — retro
                  computing, chiptunes, dead magazines, abandonware, dusty journals, and manuals for
                  chips that stopped being made decades ago. Not a full-blown internet archival
                  project; just the stuff one person didn't want to lose, kept around because it's
                  worth keeping.



                  I hoard far more than what you see here. A handful of archives get published publicly
                  as a fallback: if the original source ever goes dark, a copy is still reachable
                  here. No promises, no uptime guarantee — just a shelf that happens to be
                  online, for as long as it stays online. I homebrew everything — beer, electronics,
                  software, ideas, and apparently, archives — and I plan to keep this one up for as
                  long as I'm around to run it.



                  Gold to the few who go looking. Websites shut down,
                  servers get decommissioned, databases deprecate, and entire libraries of human work
                  quietly disappear. One shelf can't fix that — but a lot of shelves can. If something
                  here matters to you, grab it, mirror it, seed it, start your own archive.
                  Preservation scales through a lot of people doing a little of it; that's the whole
                  point of homebrew.



                  No ads. Basic analytics only. Google login. No uptime guarantee. No monetization.
                  Free for all, free forever. Information wants to be free — but
                  it also wants to be preserved.
                
              

            


            

              

                // Infrastructure
                [+]
              

              

                
                  The archive runs on a small home server cluster: two Raspberry Pi 4 (8 GB)
                  nodes for the services and scraping, plus an N150 mini-PC (12 GB) for
                  heavier indexing. They share a 5-port switch and the whole rig is fed
                  from a single 65 W USB-C power adapter. Storage is
                  4×2 TB HDDs + 2×2 TB SSDs for the hot working set, with a
                  12 TB external HDD kept offline for cold backups. The stack pulls about
                  30 W total — deliberately engineered to run off-grid on a modest solar
                  rig. No cloud, no CDN, no uptime guarantee; when the battery dies the site goes with it. Preservation
                  doesn't require a data centre — just determination, a shelf, and enough panels to cover
                  the draw.



                  Live host state in the status window
                  — subdomain reachability and process uptime, refreshed every 10 min.
